Why Structured Research?

Welcome

  • Today we cover structured literature research
  • A core skill for any Bachelor or Master thesis
  • In 30 minutes, you will learn how to:
    • Formulate a clear research question
    • Build effective search strategies
    • Use modern AI tools for discovery
    • Organize references with Zotero
    • Evaluate sources critically

Tip

Finding the right literature early saves you weeks of work later.

Common Mistakes

  • Googling randomly and hoping for the best
  • Reading only what is cited in one paper
  • Stopping after the first five results
  • Not keeping track of what you have already read
  • Relying on a single database or search engine

Warning

These mistakes lead to incomplete reviews, missed key papers, and wasted time.

A Structured Approach

  • A systematic process saves time and produces better results
  • The goal is to find, evaluate, and organize relevant literature

The process at a glance:

  1. Formulate a clear research question
  2. Search systematically across databases
  3. Snowball forwards and backwards
  4. Organize references in a manager
  5. Evaluate sources critically
  6. Write your literature review

Formulating Your Research Question

Start Broad, Then Narrow Down

  • Begin with your general topic area
  • Ask yourself: What specifically do I want to investigate?
  • Narrow down step by step

Too broad:

“Vehicle routing problems”

Focused:

“How do metaheuristics compare to exact methods for vehicle routing problems with time windows?”

Use a Framework to Structure Your Question

  • The PICO framework helps you define your question clearly
Element Meaning Example
Population What problem or domain? Vehicle routing with time windows
Intervention What approach or method? Metaheuristic algorithms
Comparison Compared to what? Exact methods (branch-and-bound)
Outcome What do you measure? Solution quality, computation time

Note

Not every research question fits PICO perfectly. Use it as a guide, not a strict rule.

Your Research Question Guides Everything

  • A well-defined question determines:
    • Which databases to search
    • Which keywords and search terms to use
    • Which papers are relevant and which are not
    • When you have covered enough ground

Important

Write down your research question before you start searching. Revisit and refine it as you learn more about the field.

Search Strategy

Google Scholar as Your Starting Point

  • Google Scholar is the most accessible tool
  • Free, broad coverage, easy to use

Pros:

  • Free and always available
  • Covers most disciplines
  • “Cited by” feature for snowballing
  • Full-text search across papers

Cons:

  • Quality varies widely
  • No advanced filtering options
  • Includes non-peer-reviewed work
  • Metadata can be incomplete

Google Scholar Tips

  • Use quotes for exact phrases: "vehicle routing problem"
  • Use the author: operator: author:taillard
  • Set date ranges to find recent work
  • Use “Cited by” to find newer papers that reference a key work
  • Use “Related articles” for similar papers

Tip

Create a Google Scholar profile and set up alerts for your key search terms. You will be notified when new papers match your query.

Building Search Strings

  • Use Boolean operators to combine search terms

AND — both terms must appear:

"vehicle routing" AND "metaheuristic"

OR — either term can appear:

"genetic algorithm" OR "simulated annealing"

NOT — exclude a term:

"optimization" NOT "continuous"

Wildcards — match variations:

optimi* matches optimize, optimization, optimizing

Putting It All Together

  • A complete search string for our example:
("vehicle routing" OR "VRP") AND
("metaheuristic" OR "genetic algorithm" OR
"simulated annealing") AND "optimization"
  • Start broad, then narrow with additional terms
  • Document your search strings so you can reproduce and refine them
  • Track how many results each search returns

Tip

Keep a search log: record the database, search string, date, and number of results for each search you run.

Snowballing

  • Snowballing is essential for comprehensive coverage

Forward citation tracking:

  • Who cited this paper?
  • Use Google Scholar “Cited by”
  • Finds newer work that builds on a key paper
  • Great for finding the latest developments

Backward citation tracking:

  • What does this paper cite?
  • Check the reference list
  • Finds foundational and seminal papers
  • Helps you understand the research lineage

Important

Combine both directions. Forward snowballing finds new work; backward snowballing finds the foundations. Together, they give you the full picture.

AI Tools for Literature Research

AI-Powered Discovery Tools

  • A new generation of tools uses AI to accelerate literature discovery
  • They complement traditional database searches
Tool What It Does
Semantic Scholar AI-powered academic search, finds related papers
Connected Papers Visual graph of related papers
Elicit AI research assistant, extracts key findings

More AI Tools

Tool What It Does
Research Rabbit Discovers related work from seed papers
Consensus AI search engine for research findings
Perplexity AI search with academic sources
  • These tools are particularly useful for:
    • Finding papers you would not discover through keyword search alone
    • Understanding how papers relate to each other
    • Getting a quick overview of a research field

How to Use AI Tools Effectively

  • Start with a seed paper you know is relevant
  • Use Connected Papers to visualize the research landscape
  • Use Semantic Scholar to find semantically similar papers
  • Use Elicit to quickly extract findings across many papers

Important Caveat

AI tools are helpful for discovery, but always verify: check the actual paper, read abstracts yourself, and do not trust AI summaries blindly. AI can hallucinate references that do not exist.

Combining Traditional and AI Search

  • No single tool is sufficient on its own
  • Use a layered approach:
  1. Start with Google Scholar and database searches
  2. Expand with AI tools (Semantic Scholar, Connected Papers)
  3. Snowball forwards and backwards from key papers
  4. Verify everything you find by reading the actual papers

Tip

Think of AI tools as a way to cast a wider net, not as a replacement for careful reading and evaluation.

Reference Management with Zotero

Why Use a Reference Manager?

  • You will read dozens to hundreds of papers
  • Without a system, you will:
    • Lose track of what you have read
    • Waste time reformatting citations
    • Miss papers when writing your bibliography
  • A reference manager helps you:
    • Organize papers by topic and project
    • Cite correctly in any format
    • Collaborate with others
    • Sync across devices

Zotero — The Recommended Tool

  • Zotero is free and open-source
  • Works on Windows, Mac, and Linux

Key features:

  • Browser connector — save papers with one click
  • PDF storage — attach and annotate PDFs directly
  • Collections — organize papers into folders
  • Tags — add custom labels for flexible organization
  • Groups — share libraries for collaborative projects
  • Sync — access your library from any device

Important

Always import via DOI when possible. This gives you the most accurate and complete metadata. After importing, always check that the data is correct.

Better BibTeX Plugin

  • Better BibTeX is essential for Quarto and LaTeX users
  • Auto-exports .bib files that stay in sync
  • Generates consistent citation keys (e.g., taillard1993)
  • Keeps your .bib file updated automatically when you add new papers

Tip

Set up Better BibTeX once and your bibliography file will always be up to date. No more manual .bib file editing.

The Zotero Workflow

A streamlined workflow for your seminar or thesis:

  1. Find a paper via Google Scholar, Scopus, or an AI tool
  2. Save it with the Zotero browser connector or import via DOI
  3. Organize it into a collection for your project
  4. Auto-export a .bib file with Better BibTeX
  5. Cite in Quarto using [@citationkey]

In your .qmd file:

Recent work by [@taillard1993]
shows that...

Rendered output:

Recent work by Taillard (1993) shows that…

Evaluating Sources

Types of Sources

  • Not all sources are created equal
Source Type Reliability Peer-Reviewed?
Journal articles High Yes
Conference papers Medium–High Usually
Preprints (arXiv) Variable No
Textbooks High Editorial review
Working papers (SSRN) Variable No
Blog posts, websites Low–Variable No

Warning

Prefer peer-reviewed journal articles and reputable conference proceedings. Use preprints and working papers with caution.

What to Check

  • When evaluating a paper, consider the following quality indicators:
  • Journal reputation — Is it a recognized journal in the field?
  • Author credentials — Are the authors established researchers?
  • Methodology — Is the approach rigorous and well-described?
  • Recency — Is the paper still current, or has it been superseded?
  • Citations — Has it been cited by others? How many times?

Note

For operations research specifically: check the benchmark instances used, the computational setup, and whether the results are reproducible.

Read Critically

  • Do not accept results at face value
  • Ask yourself:
  • Is the sample size sufficient?
  • Are the assumptions reasonable?
  • Do the conclusions follow from the results?
  • Can the findings be generalized to other contexts?
  • Are there limitations the authors do not discuss?

Tip

A good literature review is not about reading everything — it is about finding and connecting the right papers.

Wrap-Up

The Literature Research Process

Putting it all together:

  1. Question — Define a clear, focused research question
  2. Search — Use databases, Boolean operators, and search strings
  3. Snowball — Follow citations forwards and backwards
  4. Discover — Use AI tools to find related work
  5. Organize — Store everything in Zotero with Better BibTeX
  6. Evaluate — Assess quality, relevance, and reliability
  7. Write — Synthesize findings into a coherent review

Important

Start your literature research early. It is an iterative process that improves as you learn more about your topic.
